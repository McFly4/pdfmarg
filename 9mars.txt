
The commodity hedging landscape across major oil and gas companies reveals three structurally distinct approaches that reflect each company's degree of vertical integration, balance sheet size, and regional regulatory exposure.
Fully integrated majors benefit from a natural hedge between upstream production and downstream refining: when crude prices rise, upstream margins expand and offset refinery margin compression, reducing the need for aggressive financial overlays. That said, they still run formalised multi-commodity programmes under IFRS 9 or US GAAP covering crude oil, refined products, LNG, natural gas, and increasingly power, using a combination of OTC swaps, forwards, and exchange-traded futures on ICE and NYMEX. Their derivative books are large in absolute terms — often billions in gross notional — partly because their in-house trading desks generate positions that sit alongside the hedging programme and are difficult to fully disentangle in public disclosures.
Pure or predominantly downstream players face the harshest exposure profile: without upstream production to cushion margin cycles, they rely heavily on financial derivatives to stabilise refining margins. Crack spread structures are the core instrument, applied systematically on a rolling basis. European downstream companies layer IFRS 9 cash flow hedge accounting on top, routing fair value movements through OCI and isolating hedging effects from reported operating results via Clean CCS or equivalent adjustments. US independents are more aggressive in using exchange-traded futures and also manage a growing layer of regulatory compliance derivatives — carbon credits, renewable fuel certificates — that are becoming as financially significant as the commodity hedges themselves.
Large non-Western operators substitute operational scale and trading arm flexibility for formalised financial hedging. The sheer volume of crude processed and the ability to flex cargo sourcing, product slate, and delivery timing across global markets provides a form of real-options hedge that does not appear on the derivatives schedule. Financial derivatives are deployed tactically, primarily for FX management on USD-denominated trade flows, with commodity derivatives used selectively rather than systematically.
On CO₂ and emissions, the picture is consistent across all peers: ETS allowances are universally held at fair value through P&L, with no company applying hedge accounting to carbon positions. Active forward purchasing strategies on the secondary OTC market are used to smooth annual compliance costs, but the positions are speculative in accounting terms. Power price hedging only becomes material for companies with significant generation assets or retail electricity books, and is managed separately from oil and gas commodity programmes.

TotalEnergies runs one of the broadest hedging programmes in the peer set, covering crude oil, refined products, LNG, natural gas, and CO₂ allowances through OTC swaps, forwards, and exchange-traded futures under IFRS 9 cash flow hedge accounting. Shell mirrors this architecture at larger scale — $10.0bn in derivative assets and $9.6bn in liabilities at end-2024 per Note 26 — with its integrated gas and LNG trading desk (world's largest) generating significant derivative notionals across gas, power, and oil products. BP operates a comparable multi-commodity programme, though its February 2025 strategic reset toward upstream growth signals a potential reduction in low-carbon and power hedging activity going forward. Eni adds complexity through its satellite subsidiaries: Enilive hedges biofeedstock prices independently, Plenitude carries power price exposure managed at entity level, while the parent covers crude and gas in the consolidated programme.
OMV and OMV Petrom apply IFRS 9 cash flow hedges to forecast crude purchases and product sales in their refining segments, classify fair value movements as special items excluded from the Clean CCS Operating Result, and trade CO₂ OTC forwards on the secondary market without hedge accounting. PKN ORLEN disclosed the largest absolute hedging P&L impact among European mid-caps in 2024 (PLN -1.6bn settlements, PLN -2.3bn OCI reserve movement), covering crude, products, gas, and CO₂ futures on ICE Exchange. Galp runs a more selective programme centred on its Sines refinery, with upstream commodity hedges beginning to layer in as Bacalhau and Mopane production ramp.
Valero and Phillips 66 represent the most active financial hedgers in the downstream-pure space: crack spread structures (3-2-1, 2-1-1) on NYMEX/ICE are core, supplemented by NGL hedges (Phillips 66 Midstream), biofeedstock derivatives (Valero DGD), and — uniquely in the peer set — regulatory compliance instruments including RINs, LCFS credits, and EU ETS allowances for non-US assets. Chevron applies cash flow hedge accounting to forecasted crude oil sales and maintains derivatives across oil, NGL, gas, and LNG, though its upstream-dominant profile limits refining-specific hedging intensity.
Reliance Industries operates as a structural natural hedger: Jamnagar's USD-denominated crude purchases and product exports offset FX exposure at source, with financial derivatives used tactically on FX (USD/INR) and selectively on commodities. No systematic crude or products programme. Sinopec presents the most volatile derivative line in the peer set — a RMB 7,827mn swing from asset to liability between 2023 and 2024 — driven by Unipec's ~4 Mbbl/d trading volume blurring the line between hedging and proprietary positioning; CETS CO₂ disclosure remains minimal. SK Innovation relies on feedstock flexibility and SKTI's trading capabilities rather than formalised financial hedging; post-merger SK E&S integration introduces new LNG/gas price exposure (JKM/TTF, Caldita-Barossa ~1.3 Mtpa from H2 2025) that remains largely unhedged in disclosed financials; inventory effects dominate quarterly P&L swings.
Across the full peer set, CO₂/EU ETS positions are universally held at FVTPL — no company applies hedge accounting to carbon allowances — and power price hedging is material only for companies with significant generation or retail electricity exposure (Shell, Eni/Plenitude, OMV, PKN ORLEN post-PGNiG merger).
